Keywords

Community study, field experiments, social transformation, sociological intervention

Nearly a century ago, the faculty and students in the Department of Sociology at Yenching

University conducted the Qinghe Experiment, including both sociological intervention

practice and social surveys within the context of community study. In 2014, the faculty

and students in the Department of Sociology at Tsinghua University also conducted a

series of field experiments in the same area, collectively known as the New Qinghe

Experiment. In 2023, the 10th year of the New Qinghe Experiment, Li Qiang and other

leading participants published the book, The New Qinghe Experiment, which introduced

how it was designed and organized, and made an analysis attempting to reveal the mech-

anism of Chinese social transformation and find effective governance methods of local

communities.

The New Qinghe Experiment was academically inspired by the Qinghe Experiment

in the 1930s certainly, yet also differentiated by the social context of contemporary

times. First, both experiments targeted the issue of social transformation, which not

only clarifies the difference between China and the West but also identifies the internal

change of China’s idiosyncratic social structure based on the comparison between the

contemporary and the traditional. However, the New Qinghe Experiment more focused

on the latter, as it was carried out in the context of post-Danwei society ( 后单位社会),

which calls for more spontaneous public participation rather than administrative and

market-based methods to deal with governance problems (pp. 8–10). For this reason,

compared to the Qinghe Experiment’s struggle between using and breaking away from

Western mode, the New Qinghe Experiment determined to seek China’s idiosyncratic

answers according to a sense of cultural continuity imparted by social consciousness

over time. Second, both experiments were conducted and supported by government

and non-government institutions while the relationship between government and

Qinghe Experiment was organized in close cooperation between the state and society

The influence on communities brought by urbanity has long been a major topic in

sociological academia. At the beginning of the twentieth century, Western theories and

methods of community studies were introduced and practiced in China, through the col-

lective efforts of Chinese sociologists with overseas experience, represented by Wu

Wenzao (吴文藻), who ascribed localized interpretations to ‘community’ based on the

paradigmatic dichotomy of ‘community’ (gemeinschaft) and ‘society’ (gesellschaft)

coined by Ferdinand Tönnies, as well as Yang Kaidao (杨开道), Xu Shilian (许仕廉),

and their colleagues who led the far-reaching Qinghe Experiment (1928–1937), which

had resonated with Robert Park’s proposition of human ecology and the ethnographic

fieldwork by other figures within the Chicago School of sociology.

Henceforth, community studies in China, as a telling and vigorous pillar of sociology,

have long been of great significance for capturing social transformation, reflecting subtle

details of residential activities, and serving urban planning as well as policy-making (p.

4). The New Qinghe Experiment thus has taken the fruitful achievements of community

studies as one of its academic foundations, while continuing its own research of com-

munity, adopting a more flexible and inclusive perspective of understanding community

as a fluidity of social relations rather than a fixed residential neighborhood (Blokland,

2017: 17–20), to reveal the zeitgeist of contemporary China that is epitomized by Qinghe

subdistrict.

Urged by the methodological need to find effective approaches to organizing public

affairs in communities, the New Qinghe Experiment explored undiscovered traits of

Chinese society and introduced Alain Touraine’s sociology of action and Michael

Burawoy’s public sociology (pp. 368–369). Moreover, a sociological intervention was

incorporated as a tool for sociologists to engage themselves in the research target and

hence became the other vein behind the New Qinghe Experiment. The intervention

attempt was termed ‘social governance’ in the New Qinghe Experiment. It is grounded

in Chinese society with unique cultural and conceptual connotations (p. 31), which again

reveals its distinctive aim at cultivating residents as spontaneously vigorous actors, a

complement to the top-down mechanism of governmental administration.

Covering both social transformations taking place in Qinghe through in-depth com-

munity studies and possible solutions to effectively deal with public affairs and residents’

needs in the pluralistic spatial landscape of Qinghe under a government–market–society

tripartite structure (p. 76), the practices of the New Qinghe Experiment can be inter-

preted both in temporal and spatial dimensions.

On the temporal side, considering the prevalence of digital technologies (p. 205), the

New Qinghe Experiment has kept abreast of the times by developing an interactive

online map platform ‘Lu Jian Qinghe’ (Road to Qinghe, 路见清河) with the help of big

data with its peripheral applications and collaboration between the local government and

Qinghe residents, aiming to collect communal appeals for commuting promptly and

improve the efficiency of governance at local-level (pp. 211–213).

Apart from presenting major findings by using the platform, the research team also

confessed their worry concerning ‘information silo’ due to the segmentation of adminis-

trative organizations (p. 215). The anxiety was further laid out in Chapter 12 by discuss-

ing the fragmentation of the community. Compared to its original cohesiveness during

the period of planned economy, Qinghe subdistrict has transformed from a Danwei-based

overall society consisting of four regions into 17 fragmented areas that are relatively

independent and disproportionately developed (pp. 224–226, 308–309).

One typical example is Community B, a residential quarter near Factory B that has

transformed from a government-supervised and merchant-managed enterprise in the late

Qing dynasty to a defense company controlled by the Nationalist government, and then

restructured into a state-owned company after the ‘Three Great Remolding’ until recently

exploited to build high-end commercial housing (pp. 216–217). The longitudinal flow of

institutions has allowed Community B to retain many of the characteristics of a work-

unit system while simultaneously bearing a bunch of marketized elements, which are

adequately embodied in practices of grassroots commerce, such as the local mobile farm-

ers’ market. The intricacy of its history and interaction among urban management, prop-

erty management, farmer–greengrocer community, and residents’ committee has been

calling for subtler consideration and a higher level of micromanagement (pp. 262, 318).

Studies of changes in Qinghe through the lens of temporality unearth many problems

lying in public space caused by social transformations, such as the disappearance of din-

ing halls for the elderly (pp. 266–274), outflow of previous work-unit elites (pp. 317–

318), under-registration and homelessness for floating population (pp. 331–332),

outdated infrastructures, perished pipelines, proliferation of occupied parking, and lack

of ancillary facilities and property management services, and so on. (pp. 170, 210).

Thus, based on the discoveries reflected in the temporal branch, the New Qinghe

Experiment further conducted spatial-side interventions through the empowerment of

participatory actors and improvement of public affairs.

It is noteworthy that the spatiality is not only physical but also metaphysical, as stated

by social theories of space that the employment of space implies power (Blokland, 2017,

pp. 29), which can be embodied in the above triangular structure in China: policies have

the authority to define center and periphery (pp. 312–318), market has largely reshaped

Chinese housing industry through the commodification of real estate (pp. 60–61), while

the social influence is reflected in deterioration of life with concomitantly residential

conflicts in transitional communities. Since government, market, and society are closely

intertwined and have formed a governing network, the sophisticated relationships among

the three participants and the intrinsic weakness of the social side (Li et al., 2018, p. 71)

have served as the theoretical foundation for sociological interventions documented in

this book.

In response to the less satisfying situation, the New Qinghe Experiment has con-

ducted several interventions on uplifting the participatory actors in communal social

governance, typically through the establishment of the Center of Property Management,

which serves as a consulting institution and provides guidance on community services,

while incorporating a third-party organization to investigate and assess property manage-

ment services. In addition, the residents’ satisfaction was taken in as a core indicator for

evaluating the performance of property management providers in Qinghe (p. 198).

A similar operation can also be seen in Chapter 20, where a scale was developed to

construct indicators of communal happiness. The empirical results potently showed that

the operational logic behind property management or community governance goes fur-

ther beyond simple market trading, containing sophisticated relational, cultural, contex-

tual, and even emotional elements (p. 363).

In general, the New Qinghe Experiment has conducted a series of in-depth commu-

nity studies as its temporal line, reflecting the social changes that Qinghe subdistrict has

witnessed, while at the same time providing abundant sociological interventions, based

on the trilateral governance network, as its spatial line to address the livelihood issues

existed in Qinghe. The combination of describing and documenting social facts by com-

munity studies, along with reorganizing and improving society by interventions substan-

tially unveils the true meaning of the ‘experiment’ of the New Qinghe Experiment.

Other than bringing the discussion on how to make public life in communities organ-

ized under social transformation, the New Qinghe Experiment provides a springboard

for exchanging ideas on how to conduct field experiments within sociology and between

other disciplines. For a start, it once again manifests the difficulty of balancing theory-

driven and application-oriented methods in one experiment. As previous studies show, a

field experiment can not only inform social policy but also advance theory (Baldassarri

and Abascal, 2017); nevertheless, the limitation of present methods in addressing issues

of generalizability and scalability weakens both theoretical findings and intervention

effect. Even so, due to the origin and development of sociology in China, and also other

areas (Kellogg, 1914), developing theory and practicing intervention are two inseparable

goals. In the New Qinghe Experiment, resolving tensions between two goals relies on

initiators themselves to make two roles (scholar and practitioner) compatible, namely

acting the mediating effect both in a so-called naturally occurring context experiment

and in social reality (pp. 380–386). On this point, ethical issues are a concern next. As the

New Qinghe Experiment shows, the intervention planned and acted on is always non-

institutional and of contingency, which may bring about unanticipated consequences, yet

constrained by the experimental condition, the discussion on how to deal with this is not

beyond previous studies (p. 390). Finally, the New Qinghe Experiment was designed as

a longitudinal study from the very outset, which served as a way to attend to the issue of

replicability. To elaborate, the longitudinal design enables deep trust between initiators

and communities, thus rich data can be collected from varied levels and in different situ-

ations, which extends the possibility for other communities to refer to. Overall, the New

Qinghe Experiment provides a window for us to learn about community life and under-

stand the social transformation in China, and as a continuing career, it has the potential

to reveal more underlying logic of social norms and bring more valuable discussion on

methodological issues hereafter.
